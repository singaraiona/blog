A small audit of what is, today, embedded in O as a first-class DSL.

I counted five. Each is a grammar plus a handful of reactions and primitives. Each fits in roughly a hundred and twenty lines, give or take.

1. **FIX.** Tag-value financial messaging. Two grammars, one for incoming, one for outgoing. A reaction per message type. Total: a hundred and forty lines, including the venue dialect we cared about. Replaced eight thousand lines of generated Java.

2. **FpML.** XML-shaped derivatives messaging. The grammar is uglier than I would like - XML always is - but it is forty lines of PEG plus the token rules. The reactions that consume FpML for trade enrichment are another sixty.

3. **Avro.** Schema-driven binary records. The interesting bit here is that the schema is an O value at runtime; a grammar is generated from it on schema change. This is the part where "grammars are values" stops being theoretical.

4. **HTTP.** Request line, headers, body with content-length splice. The example I keep showing. Forty lines of grammar, ten of routing.

5. **A small SQL.** Selects, where, group-by, aggregates. Compiles to O verbs. Two hundred lines, more than I want, less than I expected. The O verbs already do most of the work; the grammar mostly translates.

The total is a little under six hundred lines for five protocols. It is the kind of count that makes me worry I am cheating somewhere, so I checked: the line count is real, the protocols are not stripped, and the runtime cost is the same kernel everyone else uses. The reason it fits is not that O is concise - it is, but not by that factor. The reason is that there is no boundary between the grammar, the reaction, and the kernel. They are the same dispatch.

I will keep the count and watch it. If we add a sixth protocol and the average line count holds, that is the strongest argument for the design that I have.
