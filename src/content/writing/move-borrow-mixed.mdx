---
title: move, borrow, mixed
summary: three laziness modes for join reactions. the runtime knows them. the surface still does not expose them.
publishedAt: 2022-07-12

A reaction matches a tuple of values from a tuple of reagents. What happens to those values when the body fires?

Three reasonable answers, all of which I needed in production code, and none of which I wanted to commit to once and for all at language-design time. They live in the runtime as an explicit enum:

```rust
// kernel/o/reactor/src/reaction.rs
pub enum Laziness {
    Move,
    Borrow,
    Mixed,
}

pub struct Reaction {
    pub mask: BitVec,
    pub ordering: Vec<usize>,
    pub laziness: Laziness,
    pub borrow: Vec<bool>,
    pub production: AST,
}
```

**Move.** The body consumes the matched values. They are gone from the reagents when the body returns. This is what you want for orders, for messages that should be processed once, for anything where double-handling is a bug. Only `Move` reactions can be safely rewritten by the optimiser, because their inputs are not aliased.

**Borrow.** The body sees the values but does not consume them. They remain on their reagents for other reactions to match. This is what you want for reference data: a quote that should drive ten different reactions, a snapshot that should be peeked but not popped.

**Mixed.** Some bound names are moved, others are borrowed. The `borrow: Vec<bool>` mask records which reagents in the binder list are read-only. This is what most real reactions need once they grow up.

The piece of the design I have not yet shipped is the surface. Today the user authors `react {[x:h1;y:h2] body}` and the compiler decides whether the reaction is Move, Borrow, or Mixed by looking at how the body uses the bound names. That works. It is also indirect, and the corner cases land as bugs in the analysis. I want, eventually, to expose the discipline at the binder.

The biggest source of correctness bugs in the first reactor was reactions that wanted to peek but ended up popping, leading to do-not-actually-pop guards in the body. Those guards were the worst kind of code: stateful, racy, and superficially obvious. Promoting peek-versus-pop from compiler-inferred to user-authored is the change I keep thinking about, and have not yet committed to.

When I do commit, the runtime is ready for it. The data is already there.
